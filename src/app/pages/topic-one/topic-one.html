1. The Human Body

In everyday life, we use our hands extensively. From the fingertips and knuckles to the palm of the hand and the back of the hand, our hands are versatile tools. We grip objects with our fingers and thumbs, feel textures with our fingertips, and use the wrist joint to flex and extend our hands. The nails on our fingertips protect the nailbed beneath. The web between our fingers allows for flexibility and dexterity.

Through the glenohumeral joint our hand, which is connected to our forearm and humerus is joined to the shoulders, and thus the trunk. The thorax, the chest area, encompasses the breast bone, collar bone, ribs, and the upper arms. It's here that the ribcage houses vital organs, and the axillae, or armpits, provide a junction for the upper arms. The antecubital fossa, or crook of the elbow, is where we often roll up our sleeves, revealing the intricacies of the ulna, radius, and carpus beneath.

Moving down to the lower body, the calf and thigh of the leg are powerful muscle groups, helping us walk and run. The sole of the foot is where we bear our weight, and the heel of the foot takes the impact with each step we take. The arch of the foot, made up mainly by the metatarsi, provides support and flexibility, making it essential for maintaining balance. In clinical terms, the tibia and the thigh bone (femur) make up the lower extremities, connected by the knee joint, which is protected by the patella. The pelvic bone (pelvis) houses vital organs and provides stability.

4. Idioms in Healthcare

English is rich with idioms that refer to body parts. Understanding these is crucial for empathizing with patients and understanding informal colleague conversations.

  • To keep at arm's length: To avoid being connected to someone or something.
  • On the tip of my tongue: To be almost able to remember or express something.
  • To pick someone's brain: To talk with someone to find out information from them.
  • To cost an arm and a leg: To be very expensive.
  • Get something off one's chest: To tell someone your problems or frustrations.
  • A sight for sore eyes: To be happy to see someone.
